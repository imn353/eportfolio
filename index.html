High-Performance Web Crawler

Reflection

Over 100,000 product records were scraped from the BookXcess sitemap using a 40-worker ThreadPoolExecutor on top of curl_cffi, then cleaned with BeautifulSoup into a tidy CSV. Six representative operations — load, filter, group-by, join, sort and aggregate — were then benchmarked across Pandas, Polars and PyArrow on identical workloads to quantify the impact of columnar engines and concurrency over a single-threaded baseline. First-hand experience was gained of how dramatically Python's data-processing landscape has shifted with Rust-backed libraries, and the value of measuring real workloads instead of trusting copy-pasted benchmarks was reinforced. Reproducible scripting, fair benchmarking practices and presenting results visually were all practised end-to-end.

Azure End-to-End Data Pipeline

Reflection

A fully automated end-to-end pipeline was built on Microsoft Azure to ingest customer and sales data from an on-premises SQL database into Azure Data Lake Storage using Azure Data Factory. The raw data was then transformed in Azure Databricks via PySpark notebooks following a medallion (bronze → silver → gold) lakehouse architecture, with each layer adding standardisation, business rules and aggregations. Concrete intuition was gained for how a cloud data platform is actually wired together in practice, rather than as isolated services on a marketing diagram.

03 · 2025

Big Data Handling — Benchmark

Reflection

A 4.24 GB Anime Dataset with 35 million-plus rows was loaded and processed in a Jupyter notebook in collaboration with a teammate. Loading strategies including chunked reads, sampling, lazy evaluation and column pruning were applied to keep memory under control, and Pandas, Dask and PyArrow were benchmarked across an identical workload. Out-of-core processing trade-offs were observed empirically, and the limits of "just use Pandas" thinking became visible as soon as the dataset exceeded RAM. Practical takeaways included always checking dtype memory footprints, preferring columnar formats like Parquet, and treating dataset size as a first-class design constraint rather than an afterthought.

Industry talk
TM One Industry Talk
Reflection

A clearer picture of why data engineering becomes exponentially harder as a business scales was given by the TM Talk. The comparison between a simple nasi-lemak seller and a large enterprise was used as a simple but effective way to show this, where a small business is run by one person with one process and one truth, while an enterprise is run on dozens of interconnected systems — CRM, HCM, SCM, Finance and ERP — each built with thousands of tables, complex relationships, workflows and validation rules. What stood out most was the point that the real difficulty in data engineering is rarely caused by the movement of data itself, but by the question of which system should be trusted as the "true" version of information, especially when business reality is often held outside the official systems altogether — in emails, WhatsApp chats, meeting notes, or someone's personal spreadsheet. This was reflected on as a reminder of how much real business knowledge is kept informally and left undocumented, and how systems can never fully keep pace with how fast a business actually changes. Data engineering was reframed by this talk as less about technical pipelines and more about how a business is truly operated, how conflicting versions of the truth are reconciled and how history is accurately preserved. The direction of the industry was also made clearer by the mention of unified platforms like Microsoft Fabric, Snowflake and Databricks, along with AI-powered dashboarding.
